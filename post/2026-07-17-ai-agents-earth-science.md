What is the role of AI, or an AI agent, in Earth Science? 

What a rich and dense talk we heard yesterday from Annie Britton and Julian Berman. Learning about the AI agents' progress in the field of math helps us understand the implications, the differences, and what the path will likely look like for Earth Science.

The recent breakthroughs, or the role that AI agents have been playing in math, have a lot to do with what Julian called the asymmetry of verification. In math, writing a proof is hard, but with a system like Lean, checking one is automatic. An agent can try, check itself, learn, and try again, thousands of times. It improves on its own. It's also why an agent unexpectedly closing a major open problem was enough to make the field write new norms around it, which became the Leiden Declaration.

In Earth Science, the checking is probably the hard part, if not the hardest. Certainly it's not cheap, in almost every way. We don't have that asymmetry in a way that benefits the field like it does in math. An agent can generate endless plausible outputs, but it can't cheaply tell which ones are good on its own. To make things more complicated, in Earth Science we often have multiple answers.

This led me to think about the role of scientists, and of NASA, here. As Annie put it, this work isn't diminishing us. It's making us more important. In Earth Science, building the "checker" is the scientific work: the benchmarks, the trusted observational baselines, the ways to test a model against reality. And reading the planet accurately is itself work we're still doing.
