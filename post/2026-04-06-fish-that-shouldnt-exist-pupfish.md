Death Valley didn't disappoint. It lived up to the dramatic name when I visited for the first time last week. I stood on the lowest point in North America, 86 meters (282 feet) below sea level, walked on salt (yes, I tasted a little. It was salty). I walked along the rim of a volcanic crater and saw a beautiful desert flower, desert five-spot blossoming on gravels. A golden caterpillar, sphinx moth, who apparently came out in numbers because of this year's super bloom, and an almost comical zebra-tailed lizard who ran like a puppy flagging its tail. But what surprised me the most was the fish in Death Valley. Yes, there's fish in this unforgiving, Mars-like landscape.

*Images: Ubehebe Crater, Desert Five-spot, Zebra-tailed lizard*

They are called pupfish, tiny fish that remind me of tadpoles, moving and jumping restlessly in the shallow stream. The stream is fed by ancient groundwater, slowly filtering through underground rock from distant mountains for thousands of years, and has nowhere to go but here, at the lowest point of the continent. This pupfish, which I find a rather underwhelming name for this impossible creature, can live in water four times saltier than the ocean, and temperatures reaching 40°C (104°F). They have been here for at least 10,000 years. When the vast lake that once filled this valley evaporated at the end of the last ice age, the fish that survived were the smaller ones, the ones that needed less food, less oxygen, less of everything.

*Images: Salt Creek, Pupfish*

There's so much more we want to learn from pupfish and others. The science, and the science of biodiversity, is what uncovers secrets, gives us inspiration and solutions. Did you know Pokémon was created by a boy who was called Mr. Bug? You've probably heard that Ozempic was inspired by the biology of the Gila monster. Yet we seem to hold a disproportionately large power over other species that we grant ourselves. The pupfish nearly went extinct in the 1960s because of groundwater pumping. They are the ones that survived the heat, the cold and the salt of Death Valley for 10,000 years.
